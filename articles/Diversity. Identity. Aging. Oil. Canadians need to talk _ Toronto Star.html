Diversity. Identity. Aging. Oil. Canadians need to talk

Toronto planner doing oil patch work sees need to reconcile the many directions we’re headed — such as oil dependence, an aging rural Canada — to ensure we become the advanced, mobile, tolerant society of our dreams.

We are being reinvented as our identity changes year to year, says pro athlete Tanzeel Merchant at the 2012 Summer Olympics Games.

By: Donovan Vincent News reporter, Published on Mon Jun 30 2014

Culture, identity, demographic shifts, and the impact of our oil dependence on the environment: all are key themes Canadians need to talk about on the way to answering the question of the kind of nation we are building, says architect, planner and urban designer Tanzeel Merchant.

All difficult topics to be sure, and ones we tend to avoid, says Merchant, 40, who was born in Calcutta, raised in Mumbai, and came to Canada in 2000 to do graduate studies at the University of Toronto, where he obtained his Masters in urban design.

He has lived in New Delhi and the Netherlands, and his professional life includes his current work as a consultant in Alberta, where with the regional municipality of Wood Buffalo and the local oil sands industry he’s working on developing a strategy that deals with managing the region’s growth, land use, and infrastructure challenges.

Though he has moved around he decided, at age 27, to settle in Toronto because it’s the first place he felt at home.

“Somewhere along the way I didn’t want to be anywhere else. One morning I woke up I just felt this is home. I came out late in life. This is the first place I could be at home with all the different facets of my identity — being South Asian, professional, LGBTQ. I didn’t feel like an outsider,’’ says Merchant, who became a Canadian citizen about 10 years ago.

Identity is one of those tricky subjects for Canadians, says Merchant, who knows a lot about the topic in large part because the work he does relates to how culture shapes place, and how place shapes culture.

“We use the word minority. But it’s no longer a valid term to identify people who might be different or from somewhere else, because (they) are becoming the majority,’’ he argues.

He predicts that in the next 30 years we’ll have a generation of people who’ll call this country home, and who will identify as Canadian, but who will know how to do things in a “fundamentally different way than we have ever imagined in Canada.’’

“We are being reinvented. No one can put a finger on what that new identity will be, because it changes from year to year … but I think, acknowledging that change, it’s starting to show in our elected officials, it’s starting to definitely show in the way we live our lives, the food we eat, the things we store in our shops.”

Toronto’s Scadding Court container shops, similar in approach to infill hawker stands in Asia or Africa, demonstrate how neighbourhood-owned and run micro-retail can be highly successful, profitable and affordable, he argues.

In addition, new music, words and identities are being invented daily in our urban mosaics like Regent Park. We need to embrace these approaches and take more risks, he says, adding we’re timid around these kinds of conversations.

Tied into this theme of identity are important considerations about Canada’s aging population, he says.

The urban centres will be lucky, because they’ll still have young immigrants, and relatively speaking will have enough people to sustain the quality of life that exists in those centres, he says.

But locally speaking, outside the Greater Toronto Area or Greater Golden Horseshoe, it’s going to be a “very dismal picture’’ says Merchant.

“In this context, one of the big things we’ve been grappling with here and across the nation is around transit and transportation and questions of mobility,’’ says Merchant.

“It’s the rural part of this country that will need some careful thought. They’ll continue to get older. They won’t be able to attract immigrants the way urban centres do. This has implications on economic sustainability and the quality of life there, questions that will need to be addressed head on,’’ he says.

Those conversations aren’t taking place at the moment, he says. The environment is an equally touchy subject for Canadians, Merchant argues.

One need only look at the heated debate around the controversial oil sands, a subject he is enmeshed in through his current work in Alberta.

He’s leading the development of a growth management strategy that involves Wood Buffalo, the province of Alberta, and the energy industry, and sets a 30-year planning and infrastructure framework for the region.

The oil sands resource is leading to huge local growth and tremendous change in the area, affecting settlement patterns for workers and their families, housing needs, and demands for services, concerns that are all part of Merchant’s work.

The oil sands are possibly the single largest hydrocarbon reserve on the planet, he suggests, yet right now the debate in Canada — and elsewhere, for that matter — is ‘shut it down or keep the oil sands going.’

“There’s a disconnect between the choices we’re making as Canadians and the expectations we have, whether (at the) community or government level. We don’t like the oil sands but we still want to drive cars. That oil has to come from somewhere,’’ he says.

He argues there are cleaner ways to extract oil, if we choose to be courageous enough as a government and a country to demand that. We could use taxes to reduce waste and induce efficiency, if we’re willing to look ourselves in the mirror, he adds.

“Creative ideas are already being applied in examples like British Columbia’s revenue-neutral carbon tax, and even Alberta’s levies on plastic bottles and cans. Let’s find the political courage to make models like these national standards,” he says.

The carbon tax puts a price on each tonne of greenhouse gas emitted.

B.C. says that through activities such as lowering fuel consumption, using fuels that are cleaner, increasing fuel efficiency, and adopting new technology, businesses and individuals can reduce the amount of carbon tax they pay, or even offset it altogether.

All of the carbon tax revenue will be returned to taxpayers through tax reductions, B.C. says.

Merchant believes there’s a middle ground politically and policy-wise that can achieve what this country needs.

But, “I think politicians are more inclined to manage issues, and less inclined to take on the policy realm,’’ he says.

That has to change.

“It may sound dismal and a harsh take, but I hope we’ll be able to have these conversations in a positive way, and take on these important challenges.

“I like having problems of this kind. It keeps me working,” he says.
